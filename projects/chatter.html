Chatter

A low-pressure social app concept designed to make spontaneous catchups easier than texting and less disruptive than calls.

Project Info

ClientPersonal one-person project
RoleProduct designer
Timeline1 month
ToolsFigma, Whimsical, Miro
SkillsUser research, product thinking, UI/UX mobile design, interaction design
Background

Reimagining long-distance friendship during a burnt-out moment

During the COVID-19 pandemic, I found it exhausting to maintain social connections because of Zoom fatigue and scheduling friction. Conversations either became too effortful to plan or too shallow to sustain over text.

Chatter began as a personal project to solve that tension for young adults who wanted a new way to keep in touch with long-distance friends.

Research

Calling creates connection, but coordinating it is the problem

I interviewed eight people between 18 and 25 to understand how they stayed close to friends and where their current systems broke down. Two insights stood out: people lose conversational context over time, and there is a real tradeoff between emotionally rich calls and frictionless texting.

My first idea explored micro-journaling and private updates, but user feedback made it clear that adding another communication task still felt like work. That critique led to a more promising pivot.

Pivot

From journaling app to spontaneous social room

I reframed the concept around a low-pressure way to call friends. Instead of forcing a full live conversation unexpectedly, Chatter lets users create a room, write a short description, and invite friends into a time-limited catchup.

The core features included starting a chatter, saving a chatter as metadata on a feed, joining ongoing conversations, and letting people skip and join later when they are free.

Outcome

Iteration made the concept stronger

The second concept resonated much more strongly in testing. People could imagine using it, not just admiring the idea. Feedback focused on tuning the time-limit interaction rather than questioning the product premise itself.

This project reinforced a lesson I still carry into my work: when feedback is lukewarm, it often signals a framing problem rather than a polish problem. The willingness to pivot is what made Chatter viable.
